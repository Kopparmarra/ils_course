What is ILS?

Integrated Logistics Support is a systematic approach to ensuring a system remains operational and economically sustainable throughout its entire lifecycle.

📌 Definition

ILS (Integrated Logistics Support) is a management and technical process designed to ensure a system remains operational, maintainable and economically justifiable throughout its entire life — from design to disposal.

ILS is widely used in defence, aviation, rail, energy and complex industrial sectors.

Core principle: Logistics support must be planned in parallel with system design — not as an afterthought after delivery.
🎯 Why ILS?

Without planned logistics support, a system risks becoming expensive to operate, difficult to maintain and quickly obsolete. ILS addresses this by integrating maintainability thinking from the very first design phase.

LSA & LORA Analysis

LSA and LORA are the analytical tools that drive the entire documentation effort. As a technical writer you must understand them — they are your primary input.

🔍 What is LSA?

LSA (Logistic Support Analysis) is a structured analytical process that identifies and quantifies all logistics resources required to maintain a system.

LSA starts from the system's design and maintenance concept to determine: which tasks must be performed, by whom, with which tools, spare parts and skills — and what must be documented.

For the technical writer: LSA outputs (in the form of LSAR data — LSA Record) are your primary input. They tell you exactly which maintenance tasks must be described in your manuals.
⚖️ What is LORA?

LORA (Level of Repair Analysis) is an economic and operational analysis that determines where a component is to be repaired — in the field, in a workshop, or discarded and replaced.

LORA weighs costs, availability, skill requirements and operational needs against each other for every replaceable unit (LRU/SRU).

LRU
Line Replaceable Unit — component replaced in the field (O/F level) without advanced tooling.
SRU
Shop Replaceable Unit — component repaired/replaced in workshop (D-level).
LSAR
LSA Record — database of all LSA outputs that drives documentation and training.
Discardsanalys
Part of LORA — assesses whether it is more economical to discard and replace than to repair.

The LORA process step by step

STEP 1Identify LRU/SRU — define all replaceable units in the system.
STEP 2Failure analysis (FMEA) — identify failure modes and effects for each component.
STEP 3Cost comparison — compare repair costs at O/F/D level against discard.
STEP 4Operational factors — weigh in criticality, availability requirements and operational environment.
STEP 5LORA decision — establish maintenance level per component → drives documentation and training.

Configuration Management

Configuration Management (CM) ensures documentation always reflects the actual system — a core competency for all technical writers.

⚙️ What is Configuration Management?

Configuration Management (CM) is the discipline that identifies, documents and controls the functional and physical characteristics of a system throughout its entire lifecycle.

For the technical writer this means documentation must always match the actual system configuration — correct version, correct revision, correct giltighet/applicability.

IETP & Authoring Tools

Modern technical documentation is digital and interactive. Understand what IETP means and which tools technical writers use today.

💻 What is an IETP?

IETP (Interactive Electronic Technical Publication) is a digital, interactive technical publication that replaces paper-based manuals. An IETP can filter content by configuration, display 3D models, link spare parts to drawings and guide technicians step by step.

📄 Paper-/PDF-manual
  • Static — difficult to update
  • No filtering by configuration
  • No linking to spare parts
  • No built-in warning functionality
  • Requires printing and distribution
✅ IETP (klass IV–V)
  • Dynamic — updated centrally
  • Filters by S/N or configuration
  • Linked IPB with direct ordering
  • Interactive warning messages
  • Available on tablet in the field
IETP Classes (S1000D): Class I–V where class I is simple HTML and class V is a fully interactive IETP with database connection, 3D and diagnostics support. Most defence projects target class IV–V.

Maintenance Levels — Deep Dive

Understanding maintenance levels in depth is essential — they directly govern what documentation you write, how detailed it must be and who the reader is.

O
🟦 O-level — Operator Level (Organizational Level)
Performed by the operator/crew on the system without taking it out of service. Checks, basic fault isolation, lubrication, LRU swaps. Documented in Crew Manual, Quick Reference Cards and Operator Checklist. Requires minimal technical background.
F
🟧 F-level — Field Maintenance (Field/Intermediate Level)
Performed by maintenance personnel close to the operating site, often with limited workshop resources. LRU replacements, fault isolation with test equipment, calibration. Documented in the Technical Manual (maintenance section), FIM and Maintenance Cards. Requires a trained technician.
D
🟪 D-level — Depot Maintenance (Depot/Base Level)
Performed by specialist workshops with full equipment and expertise. Repair to component level (SRU), overhaul, modifications. Documented in Overhaul Manual, Component Maintenance Manual (CMM) and Wiring Diagram Manual. Requires specialist training.
📋 Documentation per maintenance level
LevelTypical documentsReaderDetail level
O-levelCrew Manual, QRC, ChecklistOperator / crewHigh clarity, low technical detail
F-levelTM (maintenance), FIM, Maintenance CardsField TechnicianProcedure-oriented, with tools and warnings
D-levelCMM, Overhaul Manual, WDMDepot specialistHigh technical detail, tolerances, specs
Important: Always write for the right reader at the right level. O-level must be understood by an operator after minimal training. D-level manuals are technical reference documents for specialists.

📡 Saab Case — ARTHUR Mk IIThree Maintenance Levels — Three Different Documents

For ARTHUR Mk II you write three separate document packages — one per maintenance level — with radically different tone, detail level and readers:

  • O-level (Operator Manual): "Check that the cooling fin is clean and free from debris. Clean if necessary with a dry cloth." — No technical specifications. No tools.
  • F-level (Maintenance Manual): "Loosen 4× M6 screws (torque 8 Nm ±10%) with torque wrench SAB-TOOL-442. Carefully lift out TRM. Verify that connectors J3/J4 are undamaged." — Tools, torque values, warnings.
  • D-level (CMM for SPC card): "Measure voltage at test point TP-12: nominal 3.3V ±0.05V. If outside tolerance: replace U23 (LM317, Saab P/N 4510-0023-07)." — Component specifications, measurement tolerances, schematics.

Same physical system — three completely different texts. That is the essence of maintenance levels.
